WHAT I DO

Web Exploitation

    This usually begins with a remote target URL or domain that we attack over the public internet. Enumeration of all devices, services, and open ports is conducted with nmap, which will give us version information on whatever stack the target is running.
    We can then search for vulnerabilities in the specific versions of each component for exploitation with Metasploit , SQLmap, or the like. We'll find something; we always find something.
    After that, if we do not own the box outright already, we can move on to privilege escalation (PrivEsc) with tools like linPEAS (Linux local Privilege Escalation Awesome Script), winPEAS, LinEnum sh, etc, until we obtain root.
    An extensive after-action report is compiled and presented to the client along with a program of threat remediation measures, policies, and procedures.

Binary Exploitation

    The executable files that run on computers, everything from Microsoft Word on your laptop, to the Apache servers that answer your Google queries, to the bank software that keeps track of your money, they are all just binary files and vulnerable to attack, reverse engineering, and modification.
    Binary exploitation involves finding a vulnerability in a program and exploiting it to gain control of a shell or modifying that program's functions to do things it was never intended to do.
    One of the best software reverse engineering (SRE) suites for doing this is Ghidra, oddly enough, brought to us as open source software anyone can download by the glowy boys at the National Security Agency. Not kidding; they have a GitHub.

WHAT I DO, CONTINUED

Forensic Analysis

    You may not realize it, but literally every interaction you have with any screen anywhere generates log entries. That is the absolute most basic thing computers of any type do: they log things because they have to in order to just work. The sheer amount of forensic evidence we all generate every day is frankly terrifying.
    Occasionally, we are called upon to recover hidden, encrypted, or deleted information related to an investigation. This can involve personal devices, storage media, "cloud" accounts, personal servers, enterprise servers and NAS, etc.
    Pro tip: if you ever want to make sure a file is really deleted for all time, put whatever it is stored on in the microwave for 60 seconds. Short of that, we can recover pretty much anything. Again, it is just baked into the way modern electronics work.

Bug Bounties

    A "Zero Day" is a vulnerability in an application, website, or program that no one else has discovered yet -- hence, the company's engineers have had "zero days" to work on it.
    All major internet companies - Apple, Facebook, Google, all of them - have bug bounty programs designed to prevent whoever discovered the hack from selling it on the "dark web" for a truckload of cash. These are something like 35% of my income.
